Choosing the Right Model

Open vs closed, big vs small, generalist vs specialist — a decision framework instead of brand loyalty.

The skill
Model choice is an engineering decision with trade-offs, not a popularity contest. By the end you should be able to defend a pick on four axes: capability, cost, latency, and control (privacy/hosting).

What "pre-trained" buys you (and its limits)

A pre-trained foundation model arrives knowing a vast amount about language, code, and the world up to its knowledge cutoff. You get that capability instantly, for the price of an API call. But the same model has fixed limitations every AI Engineer plans around:

BenefitMatching limitation
Broad knowledge out of the boxFrozen at a cutoff date — no current events
No training cost or data neededKnows nothing private/proprietary to you (→ RAG, Lesson 25)
Strong general reasoningHallucinates confidently outside what it knows
One model, many tasksGeneric — may underperform a specialist on a narrow task

Closed vs Open-Source Models

Closed (API)Open-weight
ExamplesGPT-5, Claude Opus, GeminiLlama, Mistral, Qwen, DeepSeek, Gemma
AccessHTTP API, you rent itDownload weights, run anywhere
Top capabilityUsually the frontierClose behind, gap narrowing fast
Data privacyData leaves your perimeter (read the terms)Fully self-hosted — data never leaves
Cost modelPer-token, zero opsFree weights, but you pay for GPUs + ops
CustomizationLimited (prompts, some fine-tuning)Full — fine-tune, quantize, modify
For your trading work specifically
If options/positions data is sensitive, an open-weight model running locally (via Ollama — Lesson 22) keeps it on your machine. If you just need the best reasoning on public market concepts, a closed frontier model via API is simpler and stronger. The privacy axis often decides this before capability does.

A four-axis decision framework

  1. Capability — Does it pass your eval on your task? (Public benchmarks ≠ your use case. Build a tiny eval set — Lesson 7/8 touch on this.)
  2. Cost — Price per million input/output tokens × your volume. A 10× cheaper model that's "good enough" often wins at scale.
  3. Latency — Smaller/distilled models respond faster. Interactive UX vs batch jobs have different tolerances.
  4. Control — Privacy, data residency, ability to self-host or fine-tune.
The practitioner's move: tiered routing
Don't pick one model for everything. Route: a small/cheap model for easy/high-volume calls (classification, extraction), a frontier model for hard reasoning. OpenRouter (Lesson 28) lets you switch providers behind one API to do exactly this.

Reasoning vs standard models

A 2024–2026 split worth knowing: reasoning models (OpenAI o-series, Claude with extended thinking, DeepSeek-R1) spend extra inference compute "thinking" before answering — better at math, code, multi-step logic, but slower and pricier. Standard models answer directly — faster, cheaper, fine for most generation and chat. Match the model class to the task's reasoning depth.

Check Your Understanding

Quiz · Question 1 of 2
You must classify 5 million support tickets per month into 8 categories, accuracy-critical but not reasoning-heavy. Which choice best fits the four-axis framework?
A
The largest frontier reasoning model, because more capability is always safer here
B
A small cheap model validated on your own eval set, since volume makes cost dominate
C
Whichever model is highest on the public MMLU leaderboard this week
D
An open-weight model self-hosted, regardless of whether you have GPU ops capacity
Correct. At 5M calls/month, cost and latency dominate, and the task isn't reasoning-heavy — a small cheap model is the right class. The discipline is validating it on your eval set, not a public leaderboard. A frontier reasoning model would be massive overspend; self-hosting only makes sense if you have the ops capacity and a privacy reason.
High volume + low reasoning depth means cost is the dominant axis — a small, cheap model validated on your own eval set (option B). Frontier reasoning models overspend here; public leaderboards don't reflect your task; self-hosting needs ops capacity you may not have.
Quiz · Question 2 of 2
Your app must reason over confidential client portfolios that legally cannot leave your infrastructure. Which axis decides the model choice first?
A
Capability — pick the smartest model and worry about the data terms afterward
B
Latency — the fastest model wins because users hate waiting on portfolio data
C
Control — a self-hostable open-weight model, because the data can't leave the perimeter
D
Cost — the cheapest API wins since all providers handle confidential data the same way
Correct. A hard data-residency constraint collapses the decision: you need a model you can self-host (open-weight), so the Control axis is decisive before capability, latency, or cost even enter. Constraints that are legal/contractual usually rank first because they're non-negotiable.
A legal "data can't leave our infrastructure" rule is a hard constraint, so Control decides first — you need a self-hostable open-weight model (option C). Capability, latency, and cost only matter among the models that satisfy the residency requirement.
