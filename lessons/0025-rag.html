Retrieval-Augmented Generation

The technique that lets a model answer questions about your data. Embeddings + vector DB + generation, assembled.

The one-line definition
RAG: before answering, retrieve relevant chunks of your documents and put them in the context window, so the model generates an answer grounded in your data instead of its frozen training knowledge.

Why RAG exists

Recall from Lesson 2: the model's weights are frozen and it knows nothing private or post-cutoff. RAG is the standard fix. It directly attacks the two biggest LLM failure modes:

  • Hallucination — grounding the answer in retrieved text gives the model facts to cite instead of inventing.
  • Knowledge gaps — your internal docs, current data, and proprietary knowledge become answerable without retraining.

The pipeline (memorize this)

Indexing — done once, ahead of time

1
Chunk
Split documents into passages (~200–800 tokens) that each carry one coherent idea
↓
2
Embed
Turn each chunk into a vector (Lesson 23)
↓
3
Store / Index
Put vectors + text + metadata into a vector DB (Lesson 24)

Retrieval + generation — every query

4
Retrieve
Embed the question, fetch top-k nearest chunks (optionally filter by metadata)
↓
5
Rerank (optional)
Re-score the candidates with a cross-encoder for precision; keep the best few
↓
6
Generate
Put chunks + question in the prompt; model answers grounded in them, with citations
python — the generation step, distilledchunks = vectordb.query(embed(question), k=4)        # retrieve
context = "\n\n".join(c.text for c in chunks)
prompt = f"""Answer using ONLY the context. Cite chunk numbers.
If the answer isn't in the context, say you don't know.

Context:
{context}

Question: {question}"""
answer = llm(prompt, temperature=0.2)                  # generate
RAG is context engineering with a search step
Look at step 6: it's just assembling a good context window (your Phase 1 skill) where the relevant material is selected automatically by similarity. RAG is the "select" strategy from Lesson 16 applied to an external corpus. You already know the destination — RAG is the on-ramp.

Where RAG goes wrong

FailureCause & fix
Retrieves irrelevant chunksBad chunking (too big/small) or weak embeddings. Fix chunk size; try a better embedding model.
Right chunk retrieved, ranked lowAdd a reranker (cross-encoder) to reorder candidates by true relevance.
Answer ignores the contextPrompt the model to use only the context and to say "I don't know" otherwise; lower temperature.
Chunk lacks context to stand aloneOverlap chunks slightly; add titles/metadata to each chunk.

RAG vs Fine-tuning

A question you'll be asked constantly. RAG adds knowledge; fine-tuning adds behavior.

Use RAG when…Use fine-tuning when…
You need current/private factsYou need a consistent style, format, or skill
Data changes often (just re-index)Behavior is stable and well-defined
You need citations / traceabilityYou want shorter prompts at inference

They're complementary, not rivals — and for most teams, start with RAG (and good prompting). Full fine-tuning treatment in Lesson 29.

Frameworks

You can build RAG by hand (recommended once, to understand it). In production, frameworks remove boilerplate: LangChain and LlamaIndex are the big two; Haystack and RAGFlow are strong alternatives. They give you loaders, chunkers, retrievers, and rerankers as composable pieces.

Check Your Understanding

Quiz · Question 1 of 2
Your RAG system retrieves the correct chunk (it's in the top-10) but the answer is still wrong because that chunk ranked 8th and the model focused on chunks 1–3. What's the targeted fix?
A
Switch from RAG to fine-tuning, since retrieval clearly can't find the right chunk
B
Add a reranker to reorder the retrieved candidates by true relevance before generation
C
Raise the generation temperature so the model explores lower-ranked chunks more
D
Remove the metadata filter so more chunks are eligible for retrieval
Correct. The right chunk was retrieved — the problem is ordering, which is exactly what a reranker (a cross-encoder that re-scores candidates against the query) fixes: it promotes the truly relevant chunk so it lands in the top few the model attends to. Fine-tuning doesn't help retrieval; temperature doesn't fix ranking; loosening filters adds noise.
Retrieval found it but ranked it poorly — that's a reranking problem (option B). A cross-encoder reranker re-scores candidates so the relevant chunk rises into the top few. Fine-tuning addresses behavior not retrieval, temperature won't fix order, and dropping filters just adds noise.
Quiz · Question 2 of 2
Your app must answer questions about a knowledge base that's updated daily, with source citations. RAG or fine-tuning, and why?
A
RAG — facts change daily (just re-index) and retrieved chunks give you citations
B
Fine-tuning — bake the knowledge into the weights so no retrieval is needed
C
Fine-tuning — it's the only way to get a consistent answer format with citations
D
Neither — just use a bigger model with a longer context and paste everything in
Correct. Frequently-changing facts plus a citation requirement is the textbook RAG case: you re-index when data changes (no retraining), and because answers are grounded in retrieved chunks, you can cite the source. Fine-tuning bakes in stale facts and gives no citations; pasting an entire daily-changing KB into context is costly and doesn't scale.
Daily-changing facts + citations = RAG (option A): re-index on change, and cite the retrieved chunks. Fine-tuning would freeze stale facts and can't cite sources; and dumping the whole KB into a long context is expensive and brittle.
