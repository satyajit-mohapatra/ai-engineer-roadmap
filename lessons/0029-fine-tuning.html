Fine-Tuning

Changing the model's weights to bake in behavior. The most-reached-for, least-often-needed tool in the kit — here's when it's actually right.

Read this first
Fine-tuning is the answer to fewer problems than beginners think. Before fine-tuning, exhaust: better prompting → few-shot → RAG. Most "we need to fine-tune" instincts are solved by one of those, faster and cheaper. The roadmap places it late for this reason.

What fine-tuning actually does

You take a pre-trained model and continue training it on your examples (input→output pairs), nudging the weights so it adopts a behavior. Unlike RAG (which adds knowledge at query time), fine-tuning changes the model's default behavior — its style, format, tone, or skill at a narrow task.

The mantra (from Lesson 25)
RAG adds knowledge. Fine-tuning adds behavior. If you can phrase the need as "it should know X," reach for RAG. If it's "it should always respond like Y," consider fine-tuning.

When fine-tuning earns its keep

  • Consistent style/format the prompt can't reliably enforce, at scale (e.g. always output in your house tone or a strict domain DSL).
  • A narrow, repeated task where a smaller fine-tuned model matches a big general model — cheaper and faster per call.
  • Latency/cost — fine-tuning lets you drop long few-shot prompts (the behavior is now in the weights), shrinking every request.
  • Specialized classification/extraction with thousands of labeled examples.

When NOT to fine-tune

  • You need current or private facts → RAG.
  • You have few examples (dozens) → few-shot prompting.
  • The task keeps changing → you'd be re-tuning constantly.
  • You haven't yet tried hard at prompting → do that first; it's free.

How it works in practice

Two paths:

PathWhat you do
Hosted (OpenAI, etc.)Upload a JSONL of examples, click train, get a custom model ID you call like any other. Easiest entry.
Open-weight (LoRA / PEFT)Use LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) to train a small set of adapter weights instead of the whole model — cheap enough to run on modest GPUs. The standard modern technique.

LoRA, in one line
Instead of updating billions of weights, LoRA learns a few small "adapter" matrices that adjust the model's behavior — orders of magnitude cheaper to train and store, with most of the benefit. It's why fine-tuning open models is now accessible, not a data-center-only luxury.

Check Your Understanding

Quiz · Question 1 of 2
A team wants the model to "know our latest internal pricing, which changes weekly." They propose fine-tuning monthly. What should you advise?
A
Fine-tune weekly instead of monthly so the pricing is always fresh in the weights
B
Use RAG — facts that change weekly belong in retrieval, not baked into weights
C
Fine-tune once on all historical pricing so the model can interpolate future prices
D
Raise the model's temperature so it adapts to new pricing automatically
Correct. This is a knowledge need with frequently-changing facts — the textbook RAG case (Lesson 25). Fine-tuning bakes facts into weights that go stale immediately, and re-tuning weekly is slow, costly, and fragile. Put the pricing in a retrievable store and let the model fetch the current value at query time.
Frequently-changing facts = RAG (option B), not fine-tuning. Weights go stale the moment pricing changes; re-tuning weekly is wasteful; models can't "interpolate" future prices; and temperature has nothing to do with knowledge freshness.
Quiz · Question 2 of 2
You have 5,000 high-quality examples of your exact house writing style and need every output in that style, with shorter prompts to cut cost. Which technique fits, and what's the cheap modern way to do it on an open model?
A
RAG — retrieve style examples and paste them into every prompt at query time
B
Fine-tuning, done cheaply with LoRA adapters rather than full-weight training
C
Few-shot only — never fine-tune, just keep adding more examples to the prompt
D
Higher temperature, which makes the model match a consistent house style
Correct. Consistent behavior/style at scale, with thousands of examples and a goal of shorter prompts, is the canonical fine-tuning case — and LoRA is the cheap modern way to do it on open weights (train small adapter matrices instead of the whole model). RAG and few-shot would keep stuffing style into every prompt (the opposite of the cost goal), and temperature doesn't impose a style.
This is a behavior/style need at scale with a cost goal — fine-tuning, done cheaply via LoRA (option B). RAG and few-shot put examples in every prompt, contradicting the "shorter prompts" goal; temperature doesn't create a consistent house style.
